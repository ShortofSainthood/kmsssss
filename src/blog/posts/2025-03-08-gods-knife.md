I've realized that a lot of my recent art isn't stuff that I'd feel comfortable just putting in a gallery page. A lot of the drawings are related to each other and warrant more context than I could give them in a little description box.

In situations like these, I plan to occasionally post **artlogs**, blog posts that go into detail about the creative process. These will also probably include more works-in-progress, so that my gallery page can be home to my finished works.

To start off, I wanted to talk about an idea I had about a year ago.

<!-- excerpt -->

In July/August of 2024, I had what I'm going to generously call a "creative frenzy". I had this idea for a story-driven comic and I wanted to get as much done as humanly possible before the frenzy went away. Unfortunately, I don't think a full-fledged project like this will ever be in the cards for me. I think I just have to accept that I'm more of an illustrator than a comic artist, and I just don't have the time, energy, focus or skill for writing long plots or character arcs.

That said, I'm still glad I got down what I could. If nothing else, it's good for getting your emotions out. Maybe I'll rework the idea some day. For now, I just wanted to share what I had done.

**Kit Katerson** (working name) is a down on her luck, minimum wadge worker, who struggles with her sense of identity and forming her own opinions. Specifically, she feels like a lot of her thoughts and feelings contradict each other, and she doesn't know how to reconcile with that. In the end, she ends up just bottling them up.

In-between her day job, she frequently signs up for clinical trials. It's not super pleasant, but it helps her pay the bills. She's pretty blasé about the whole thing, and emotionally underreacts to my negative effects she experiences. Overtime, she starts applying more risker and risker trials, until one day the doctors tell her about a top secret project called **Operation: God's Knife**. It's incredibly risky, but she's already been through a lot and this pays more than anything she's ever done, so she goes through with it. How bad could it be?

Kit is hooked up to a giant machine. The doctors turn the machine on, and all of a sudden Kit is struck with the most excruciating pain she's ever felt. It starts in extremely precise points across her body and slowly spreads, as if she was being ripped apart atom-by-atom. She feels her body becoming more looser, almost jelly-like. At some point she losses consciousness.

The Kit that wakes up later is not make the same Kit who passed out. She is no longer a **whole person**, both her body and her consciousness has been **split in half**! The doctors are ecstatic, calling it a "medical miracle", but the newly-formed Kits aren't as amused.

The rest of the story has to do with "Kit" navigating everyday life, now that the two sides of herself are their own people with different thoughts, feelings, and goals. Her internal conflicts, now externalized. One binges, the other restricts. One is ambivalent about their body, the other is desperately trying to control it. One wants to forgive anyone whose ever hurt them, the other wants revenge.

The two parts aren't permanently physically separated. They aren't made of the same kind of solid matter as other living beings. They're able to sort of morph in and out of each other in this weird cartoony way, appearing to be "whole" again. Kind of like two blobs of slime absorbing each other, but more solid. It's not like how it was before, though. If one part disagrees with the other, they can unintentionally separate or phase out of the shared body.

At first, they try to morph together and pass as a singular Kit. They never leave the other one alone and try to make compromises for each other. However, as time goes on, the Kits grow into their own individual people, making it harder for them to work together as one person.

Despite that earlier summary, I didn't really plan for this to be a straight up sci-fi story. The medical/science elements were really just a catalyst for the story. The real meat of it was planned to be more of a character study. What does it mean to be a complete person? How does one part grow when the other is constantly pulling them down? How to outsiders react to this sudden change?

The art I made for it isn't too impressive. I think I was more concerned with just getting my ideas on paper than I was making them perfect.

I couldn't decide how cartoony I wanted the art to be. I find it easier to draw less detailed art, but I felt like omitting certain details would do the story a disservice. For example, a lot of the themes I wanted to cover have to do with body image and bodily autonomy, which is harder to convey when all the characters have doll anatomy. In some drawings, characters have breasts or top surgery scars, but in others they don't.

Not exclusive to this project, but I made most of the art using the [Vibrant 14 Palette](https://lospec.com/palette-list/vibrant-14). I should probably talk about this more in a separate post, but I *love* using limited color palettes for my art. It also just makes things more visually cohesive when all characters from a given project use a shared color palette.
